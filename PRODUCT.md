# Product

## Register

product

## Users

The primary user is a beginner chess learner who has watched opening videos but struggles to recall the right moves when facing a real board. They want to practice the London System as White and the Caro-Kann as Black in a setting that feels close to playing a game, with guidance available at the exact moment memory fails.

The product is for focused solo practice. The user may be practicing for casual chess.com or Lichess games, reviewing recurring mistakes, or trying to turn opening ideas into habits rather than memorized video fragments.

## Product Purpose

Chess Opening Tutor helps a beginner learn two practical openings by making them play the moves on an interactive board while a tutor explains what to do, why it works, and how to recover when an opponent leaves the memorized line.

Success means the user can:

- Recognize common London System and Caro-Kann positions from real games.
- Choose the next opening move without needing to rewatch a video.
- Explain the plan behind the move in beginner-friendly language.
- Recover from common opponent deviations using principles, not panic.
- Review missed positions until they become automatic.

## Brand Personality

The product should feel patient, precise, and confidence-building. The tutor should be user-selectable, with a calm coach as the default and additional styles available for users who prefer a stricter trainer or a lighter companion.

The interface should feel like a practice room: quiet, readable, responsive, and board-centered. It should not feel like a dense chess database, a corporate analytics dashboard, or a childish game.

## Anti-references

Avoid:

- Dense opening databases full of notation tables, unexplained ECO codes, and master-level assumptions.
- Video-first lesson structures where the user watches passively.
- Generic SaaS dashboards with metrics cards as the main experience.
- Over-gamified learning surfaces that distract from board practice.
- Engine-analysis overload that shows centipawn details before the user understands the plan.
- Long prose panels that make the user read instead of play.

## Design Principles

1. Practice before theory: every lesson should quickly ask the user to make a move on the board.
2. Explain plans, not just moves: tutor feedback should tie moves to piece placement, pawn breaks, and typical opening goals.
3. Reduce beginner panic: when the opponent deviates, the app should map the position back to a known plan or explain the nearest principle.
4. Keep the board central: notation, hints, progress, and commentary should support the board rather than compete with it.
5. Make recall visible: missed positions should return through review until the user can play them confidently.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

The product should meet WCAG 2.2 AA expectations for color contrast, keyboard navigation, focus states, semantic structure, and reduced-motion preferences.

Specific accessibility requirements:

- The chessboard must be operable without drag-and-drop.
- Board coordinates, move history, and tutor messages must be screen-reader friendly.
- Color choices for pieces, highlights, and arrows must not rely on color alone.
- Motion should help orientation, but core learning must work with reduced motion enabled.
- Text should stay readable on phone, tablet, and desktop layouts.
